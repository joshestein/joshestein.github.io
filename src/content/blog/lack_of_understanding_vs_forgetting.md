Ah yes. That familiar feeling of stumbling upon a piece of code you don't
understand and asking yourself "WTF is this?". Then realising that you were the
one who wrote it and it makes no sense. What the hell were you thinking?

This is part of why comments are so useful. Because reading code from a past
version of you is the same as reading code from a complete stranger. You won't
remember why you made the choices you made, or what the context was for some
design decision. Only the comments from the ghosts of your past will be able to
whisper through your terminal to you.

Is this really so different from having large parts of a codebase be opaque to
you because they were written by an LLM? People are yapping on about the danger
of 'not understanding the code that the AI wrote'. I'd argue that you never
really understand all of the code in your system.

Of course, if you study something you've previously written it'll likely come
back to you. At the same time, you could ask an LLM to explain a part of your
codebase that you don't understand.

The fundamental difference in your mastery of this part of your codebase might
be different. Of course, when you use an LLM, your understanding will be
shallow.

But is it really necessary to understand _all_ of the pieces of a system with
rigour and depth?

I'm never one to advocate for ignorance. And I certainly wouldn't advocate for
not understanding the fundamental technologies you're building with, or
important design decisions. But the intricacies of the styling of a particular
component? Or the exact format a particular API request needs? Does it matter
if it was written by a past version of yourself now forgotten or an LLM where
you didn't watch it like a hawk?
